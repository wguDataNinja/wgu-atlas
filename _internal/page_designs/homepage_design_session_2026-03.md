## Purpose of this doc

This document preserves the higher-level homepage conclusions from the March 2026 design session.

It is not a final wireframe and not an implementation checklist.

Its role is to preserve:
- what the homepage needs to communicate
- what the strongest product surfaces are
- how the raw catalog baseline changes homepage priorities
- where ecosystem material fits
- what kinds of homepage directions are supported or not supported by the evidence

---

## Central conclusion

The homepage should not behave like a thin entry point to the rest of the site.

It should behave like a proof surface for what Atlas makes possible.

That conclusion became much stronger after reviewing:

- the raw catalog structure
- the Atlas BSCS degree page
- the compare page
- the actual source-vs-product transformation

Atlas is doing more than browsing catalog data.
It is restructuring a fragmented source system into a student-usable guide.

The homepage should show that.

---

## What changed during this session

At the beginning of the session, the homepage could still be described as a mostly navigational entry surface.

After reviewing the source baseline and the strongest inner pages, that framing became too weak.

The evidence now supports a stronger product story:

- the raw catalog is fragmented
- degree understanding is split across multiple sections
- compare is absent from the source
- course understanding requires repeated lookups
- history/change context is absent
- official supporting resources are not attached to degree entries

Atlas addresses all of those problems.

That means the homepage can and should present Atlas as a transformed-information product, not just an independent guide.

---

## Strongest product surfaces identified in this session

### 1. Degree pages
Why strong:
- description, outcomes, roster, history, and official resources can all appear on one page
- they solve a real source fragmentation problem
- they are visually substantial near the top of the page
- they communicate catalog structure better than the source does

Homepage role:
- must be prominently featured

### 2. Compare
Why strong:
- the raw catalog has no comparison affordance
- Atlas Compare is a real new capability
- the compare UI is visually legible and screenshot-friendly
- it is one of the clearest demonstrations of modeling and product judgment

Homepage role:
- must be prominently featured
- probably more prominently than in the current homepage

### 3. Course pages / course linking
Why strong:
- the source requires separate alphabetical lookups for course descriptions
- Atlas turns roster course codes into navigation paths
- this removes a major source-format limitation

Homepage role:
- likely featureable, though below degree pages and compare

### 4. History/change context
Why strong:
- this does not meaningfully exist in the source catalog
- Atlas adds first-offered dates, CU-change context, school-name history, and other continuity signals

Homepage role:
- can be surfaced as a secondary differentiator

### 5. Relevant Resources / official-resource curation
Why strong:
- the source catalog does not attach official supporting resources to program entries
- Atlas surfaces those resources directly on degree pages

Homepage role:
- likely a supporting proof point rather than a lead card

## Current homepage diagnosis

The current homepage is functional but under-demonstrative.

### What it currently does well
- provides search
- provides school navigation
- states the site is an independent guide
- provides a path to Compare

### What it currently underplays
- degree-page richness
- compare as a flagship feature
- course linking value
- archive/history intelligence
- scale of the site
- official-resource curation
- broader structured-research work

### Most important weakness
The current homepage is closer to a navigation shell than a product showcase.

It does not yet make the strongest site work obvious within seconds.

---

## What the raw catalog baseline changed

The catalog analysis changed the homepage discussion substantially.

Before that analysis, degree pages could be described mainly as useful detail pages.

After that analysis, degree pages are better understood as:
- consolidation surfaces
- source-fragment-repair surfaces
- a stronger argument for Atlas's value than simple browsing pages usually are

Similarly, Compare became even more important because it is not just useful. It fills an outright missing capability in the source.

This means the homepage should likely lead with transformed student tasks, such as:
- understand one degree in one place
- compare related degrees directly
- open any course from the plan
- see more than a static snapshot

---

## Homepage framing direction

The homepage should communicate some version of this:

Atlas restructures WGU's catalog and surrounding official context into something students can actually use.

That is a stronger and more defensible public framing than:
- browse degrees and courses
- independent guide to WGU
- catalog explorer

Those are true, but they undersell the site.

---

## What the homepage should show, not just say

The homepage should visually demonstrate at least the following:

### A. Degree-page consolidation
Show that one page contains:
- description
- term-structured roster
- outcomes
- history
- official resources

### B. Compare capability
Show that related degrees can be compared side by side with shared vs unique courses visible.

### C. Course connectedness
Show or imply that courses are directly explorable from rosters.

### D. Broader intelligence
Optionally hint that Atlas includes:
- historical program context
- official-resource curation
- wider WGU ecosystem understanding

The first two are the essential proof modules.

## Homepage directions considered during the session

Several directions were discussed conceptually.

### Product-first
Good discipline, but risks under-showcasing the strongest work.

### Showcase-first
Stronger match for the actual product strengths.
Can work if grounded in student usefulness rather than self-promotion.

### Search/task-first
Useful, but can underplay how differentiated Atlas is.

### Ecosystem-augmented
Interesting, but risky if ecosystem material starts competing with core academic-product identity.

### Split model (Atlas first, ecosystem later)
Promising compromise:
- feature Atlas itself prominently
- keep broader WGU ecosystem material below the fold or secondary

---

## Recommended homepage direction from this session

The homepage should move toward a showcase-oriented but student-useful structure.

A likely good direction is:

1. Hero with search and stronger value statement
2. Proof modules for:
   - Degree Pages
   - Compare
   - Course Pages (or course-connectedness)
3. College/school navigation
4. Archive/history or "more than one catalog snapshot" proof
5. Around WGU / ecosystem section lower on page
6. Trust / "how Atlas works" light explanation
7. Footer

This preserves a strong core product identity while allowing room for broader ecosystem work later.

---

## Ecosystem material — role in homepage

The session did not conclude that ecosystem material should dominate the homepage.

However, given the amount of research and curation already done, it likely deserves some presence.

Best current role:
- secondary or lower-page section
- clearly separated from the core Atlas academic-reference story
- framed as curated official/unofficial orientation, not a giant directory

It should not displace:
- degree pages
- compare
- course-connectedness

Those remain the homepage's strongest proof surfaces.

---

## What the homepage should not do

### Do not lead with datasets/methods
These are secondary and power-user-oriented.

### Do not lead with ecosystem material
That would misstate the site's core value.

### Do not rely on abstract explanation alone
The strongest evidence is visible product transformation.

### Do not bury Compare
It is one of the clearest unique features.

### Do not underuse degree-page proof
Degree pages became more important once the source baseline was understood.

## Core messaging themes supported by this session

The following themes are now well-supported by evidence:

- understand a degree in one place
- compare related degrees directly
- open any course from a degree plan
- see more than a static catalog snapshot
- find official WGU resources that matter
- use a structured guide instead of flipping through a fragmented catalog

These messages are stronger than generic exploration language.

## Recommended teaser priorities

### Highest priority teaser
Compare

Reason:
- unique capability
- strong screenshot candidate
- easy to understand
- easy to sell truthfully

### Co-anchor teaser
Degree pages

Reason:
- richest consolidated surface
- clearest source-vs-Atlas transformation
- strongest "everything useful in one place" argument

### Third teaser candidate
Course pages / course linking

Reason:
- solves repeated lookup friction
- demonstrates connected site graph

### Secondary teaser
History/change context or Relevant Resources

Reason:
- strong differentiator, but not as instantly legible as Compare or degree pages

## Why the session matters

This session moved the homepage discussion from:
- "how should the homepage be arranged?"

to:
- "what is the strongest truthful claim the homepage can make about Atlas?"

The answer is no longer just navigation or browsing.

The strongest truthful claim is that Atlas turns a fragmented source system into a structured student-use product.

That should shape all later homepage work.

---

## Preservation summary

This design session established the following durable conclusions:

1. The raw catalog baseline makes Atlas more impressive than the current homepage communicates.
2. Degree pages and Compare are the two strongest homepage-proof surfaces.
3. Course-connectedness is more important than it first appeared.
4. History/change context is a real differentiator, not decorative enrichment.
5. Relevant Resources are meaningful because the source does not attach them.
6. Homepage redesign should become more demonstrative and less purely navigational.
7. Ecosystem material belongs later in the page or as a secondary section, not as the lead identity.
